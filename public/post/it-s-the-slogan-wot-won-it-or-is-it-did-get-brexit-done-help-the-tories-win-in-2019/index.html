    Was It The Slogan Wot Won It?

    Posted at — Jan 9, 2020 DRAFT

    [TL;DR: Probably not.]

    “Get Brexit Done.” Those were “the three words that helped Boris Johnson win” Britain’s 2019 election, according to Time Magazine.

    The slogan was a “golden key” that unlocked a Conservative victory, says the Telegraph.

    Not to be outdone, this Spectactor columnist thinks it was a “work of of political genius”, with “power, more power than us smug, clever types who obsess about details and facts naturally recognise”.

    Even prominent Labour figures like John McDonnell said that the slogan had “successfully resonated” with voters.

    What these analyses share – aside from an occasionally breathless tone – is a strange conception of what politics is…

    We might call it politics as electoral spellcasting1: the idea that if you can just find the right sequence of magic words – Take Back Control, Get Brexit Done – you can “resonate” your way to electoral victory.

    If you think I’m being uncharitable in this depiction, witness how the media has depicted Dominic Cummings (Vote Leave campaign chief and now advisor to Boris Johnson): an evil Svengali, a master of the dark arts, or (my personal favourite) the Demon Headmaster?2

    So… is this narrative true?

    Did the “Get Brexit Done” slogan actually affect the election result?

    Do campaign slogans really have the power to change minds?

    Reasons for skepticism

    1. Fundamentals

    One big reason for being skeptical about campaign effects is that we can do a pretty big job at predicting election results just using information from before the campaign starts.

    Typically the predictors we use are “fundamental” factors like the state of the economy and the government’s approval rating, measured pre-campaign.

    What’s really striking is that even in the age of waning party attachments and (supposedly) increasingly sophisticated campaigns, fundamental factors do as good a job in predicting election results now as they did 50 years ago [1].

    2. Experimental evidence

    If we really want to know whether something has a causal effect, then, just like with a medical trial, we need to do a controlled experiment.

    How does this work for political “treatments” instead of medical ones? Well, typically a researcher will partner with a political candidate or party, and convince them to randomize some aspect of their campaign - e.g. by flipping a coin to decide where to send your door-to-door canvassers, or where to air your TV ads, or where to send your leaflets.

    A big difficulty here is that campaigns vary a lot! Some canvassers are more persuasive than others. Some candidates might just have the right message for the right time. So rather than relying on any one particular study, we want to sort of “average” over a bunch of existing studies.

    This is called a “meta-analysis”. Strikingly, two big meta-analyses of persuasive campaign effects find that our best guess is that the effects are zero [???, @coppock2019persuasive]

    lots of other studies claiming to find big effects relating to subtle linguistic cues (e.g. describing someone as a “voter” increases likelihood of voting??) have been debunked: - ??? big replication shows that subtle linguistic cues (describing the recipient as a “voter”) don’t have huge turnout effects! - lots of results have been debunked… ??? finds no difference between -ve or +ve framing on norms

    When we do observe large effects, they decay very quickly. ??? shows that campaign mobilization effects decay very quickly

    1. availability bias. campaign messaging is often eye-catching and memorable. whether it’s a clever word play, a slightly silly promise (Get Brexit Done) or just completely outrageous, they stick in our minds. but this does not mean they change our minds! rather, it means that when we want to explain an apparent shift in voter preferences, these messages are easily accessible. A stonking Tory victory in 2019? Well, “Get Brexit Done” clearly resonated.

    evidence that individual slogans tend to get overblown - e.g. death panels written about extensively in media but not used very much by politicians and almost never volunteered by voters when talking about healthcare.

    3. Competing messages

    4. Backfire

    another reason campaigns might not work is that… they’re annoying?

    ??? having an elected official email highlighting a particular issue does not increase issue salience - in fact it seems to lead to backfire efffects where citizens downgrade the highlighted issue and express LESS interest in signing a petition about it!!

    It can even backfire - one study found that voters who were randomly assigned to receive a “persuasive” pro-Obama message on their doorstep were actually less likely to vote for Obama [3].

    OK so… do campaigns matter at all?

    probably yes! one reason for null effects in field experiments is that you are competing with lots of other messages. zero net effect does not mean zero direct effect! imagine a tug of war…

    so far we’ve focused on persuasion - e.g. vote for me, not the other guy. that’s hard. but campaigns may be more effective when it comes to mobilization (who turns out) and priming (which issues are seen as most important).

    What does political science say about campaign effects?

    Political scientists have long been skeptical about whether political campaigns actually have an effect on voters, for one simple reason.

    We can predict election outcomes surprisingly well based on “fundamental” factors like the state of the economy and vote intentions that are measured well before the campaign has started.

    Even in recent years, when party attachments are waning, voter volatility is rising, and campaigns are (supposedly) becoming more sophisticated, fundamental factors like the economy are still just as good at predicting elections now as they were 50 years ago [1].

    You can get a pretty good sense of how this debate has evolved just by glancing at the titles of a few books written on this subject…

    • Do Campaigns Matter? (1996)
    • The Message Matters: The Economy and Presidential Campaigns (2009)
    • The Timeline of Presidential Elections: How Campaigns Do (and Do Not) Matter (2012)

    What is “the campaign”?

    What do we actually mean by “the campaign”?

    It might be useful to break it down into two components: content and contact.

    Content is the message: everything from which issues the campaign chooses to emphasize, down to specific slogans.

    slogans, That includes things like slogans, images, and emphasisis on some issues over others.

    Contact is how the campaign actually reaches voters. That includes leaflets, door-to-door canvassing, TV adverts, as well as “indirect contact” via the media (think: leadership debates, press coverage of campaign rallies).

    What do political campaigns (aim to) do?

    • Mobilization: changing who turnouts on election day.

    This could be broad-based (e.g. civic GOTV campaigns) or partisan (increase turnout among your likely voters, decrease among your opponent’s likely voters).

    • Persuasion: changing who they vote for
      1. Direct persuasion: change vote choice by directly changing voter’s preferences
      2. Priming: change vote choice by changing issue salience
      3. Informing: change vote choice by changing factual beliefs (e.g. about the current unemployment rate)

    Persuasion is expensive because it takes resources to actually reach voters

    And because the effects wear off, fast! One study from a 2006 gubernational race in the US found that a randomly-assigned weekly ad buy could raise the candidate’s support by up to 5 percentage points - but this effect had disappeared by the following week [4].
